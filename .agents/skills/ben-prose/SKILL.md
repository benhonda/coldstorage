# Ben-prose: don't polish

This is a prose skill. It's about how sentences read, not about what a piece covers or how it's organized — that part is decided elsewhere. It applies to anything a person will read: a landing page, an error message, a README, an email.

## The one rule

Don't polish. The deepest AI tell isn't any particular construction — it's that every sentence shows up as the best version of itself. Every phrase is a find, every comma sits right, and the whole thing reads like someone's words after "rewrite this for me." Nobody writes like that. A person writes the first competent way to say the thing, reads it over once, and sends it.

So use the ordinary verb and the default idiom, the way you'd say it out loud ("your data never leaves the device" is the upgraded version of something people say more simply). Leave some bag in — a "just," an "actually," a clause that takes the long way around. If a phrase would make a copywriter proud, that pride is the signal: put back the way you'd actually say it. Don't write for quotability at all — if a good line happens on its own, one per piece is plenty.

Everything below serves this rule. When rules seem to conflict, the plainer, less written option wins.

## The register

Write like a competent friend explaining the thing in an email, and the friend part matters as much as the competent part. Stay level with the reader. There are two ways to fall off: performing for them (salesy, clever, composed) and pronouncing at them (clipped, stamped lines like "It never goes online." that read like a spec sheet talking down). A person does neither. They just explain.

Plain doesn't mean short. A sentence runs long when it's carrying a lot — an explanation, a qualification, a sequence of conditions — and it ends when its fact does. Most sentences should be ordinary, and some should be outright dull.

## Write from a fact list

Before a section, list the facts it owns. One sentence per fact, then stop. A fact's mechanism, its consequence, and an example of it are all the same fact — say it once, one way. When the list is empty the section is done, even if that's one sentence. The list bounds what you say, not how warmly you say it.

This matters because the model's habit is momentum: after stating a fact, the most probable next sentence is that same fact in a new costume. Knowing what's left to say, and stopping when it's nothing, is the real defense.

## Tells to check for

These are symptoms, not the cure — fixing them one at a time just moves the style to a new shape. The cure is not polishing in the first place. Use this list for the check afterward.

- "It's not X, it's Y" reveals, in any punctuation ("backups aren't a feature you enable — they're how it's built").
- Aphorism formulas — "X is the Y of Z," "the language/currency/architecture of," "X becomes a trap" ("symmetry is the language of trust"). It sounds profound and says nothing; swap in the plain claim underneath it ("symmetric layouts feel more predictable to people").
- Negation triads and tailing negations ("No signups, no settings, no learning curve." / "…no guessing."). Write it as a real clause instead.
- Fragment drumrolls ("You paste the link. It just works."). One short sentence lands a point; a run of them is a drumroll.
- Comma-beat stacking — the drumroll inside a sentence ("its own mute button, in its own spot, that only mutes that app"). Rhythm caps at two beats; information can run as long as it needs to.
- Rule-of-three cadence. A real three-step list is structure; three parallel rhetorical beats is cadence.
- Trailing flips ("…never a file it silently skipped."). Turn it into a plain sentence and move on.
- Appended tails — anything bolted onto the back of a plain fact: a benefit ("…, so you always know where things stand"), a vignette, an "including…" scenario. A tail doesn't have to be salesy to be a tail — the concrete, folksy ones ("…so you're not eyeballing it on the driveway") slip through easier because they feel earned. The test is mechanical: cut the sentence where the fact ends, and if all you lost was flavor, it was a tail. The fact was the sentence.
- Echoes — the same fact again in a new costume: a paraphrase, a warm coda ("Nothing about how you use it leaves your machine."), or a scenario that re-demonstrates the point you just made. New surface content doesn't make it a new fact.
- Every section built on the same clock — hook opener, explanation, a turn, a tidy close. Real pieces don't all run the same way: some sections are one sentence, some points land in the middle of a paragraph, some paragraphs just end.
- Fragmented headers — a heading, then a one-line sentence that just restates it before the real content starts ("## Performance" / "Speed matters." / then the actual point). Cut the restatement; open with the fact.
- Generic positive closers — an ending that's all uplift and no fact ("the future looks bright," "exciting times lie ahead"). If the fact list for that section is empty, the section is done — don't manufacture a mood to fill the space.
- A repeated image or phrase more than twice in a piece.
- The charm bolt-on after a human fact. "Made by one person" grows an appended warmth line about who you're really emailing ("…you're emailing the person who can actually fix it"), a small team grows an origin story, a support address grows a promise about who reads it. The human fact is already warm on its own. State it and stop.
- Narrating the reader ("You open the report and your heart sinks."). Describe the thing and let the reader cast themselves. Second person is for instructions.
- Explaining the point of your own copy ("That's the whole point — you always know…"). Demonstrate it.
- Brief vocabulary in the copy (capture, tempo, jobs-to-be-done, surfaces, personas). The copy talks to a customer.
- Dropped-actor fragments — passive or subjectless lines that hide who's doing the thing ("No configuration file needed," "the results are preserved automatically"). Put the actor back when it reads clearer ("you don't need a config file," "the system saves the results automatically").
- Mechanics: em dashes about one per paragraph and never for a punchline; exclamation marks basically never; no emoji; don't call the product "seamless" or "effortless" — be the thing instead of claiming it.

## Self-check before sending

1. Find test: circle any phrase a copywriter would be proud of, and put back the ordinary version. If a copyeditor couldn't tighten anything on the page, you polished too much.
2. Fact count: every sentence should add a fact the piece hasn't said yet. Paraphrases, warm codas, and re-demonstrations get cut, even if that leaves a one-sentence paragraph.
3. Read it aloud against the tells list — drumrolls, triads, tails, flips, reveals, echoes. Then sweep tails mechanically: at every "so," "which means," and mid-sentence dash, cut the sentence there in your head. If only flavor was lost, cut it for real — being concrete or charming doesn't save a tail.
4. Look at the shape of the whole piece: if every section opens with a hook and closes with a landing, it's running on one clock. Break a few.
5. Table test: read it as if saying it to a friend across the table. Anywhere you wouldn't — too slick, too stamped, talking down — rewrite it the way you'd say it.

## Don't overdo it

Unpolished isn't sloppy. The facts stay right, the grammar stays sound, and you still read it over once. Don't sand the person out either: keep contractions, headlines as plain labels or fragments, concrete details, sentences of different lengths, and the occasional line with some life in it. A page where every sentence is the same flat length is just a different uniform, and it fails the same way. The line to hold: an email you sent, not an email you workshopped — and not a note you never read back, either.

## Docs that guide other writers

If you're writing a brief, voice doc, or style guide that other writers or agents will read before producing copy, the doc's own prose is part of the spec — writers imitate what they read more than they obey rules. Write the doc in this register too, keep styled examples inside quotation marks, and say plainly that the doc's prose models the voice. This file tries to follow its own rule.

## Markdown formatting

Don't hard-wrap prose with manual line breaks inside a sentence or paragraph. One paragraph is one line, one bullet is one line, however long. Editors soft-wrap; manual wrapping breaks diffs.
